What is Section 106?

Section 106 is a part of the larger National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA) legislation that was enacted in 1966. Section 106 requires undertakings that are on federal land or employ federal funding to take into consideration the effect of the undertaking on affected historic properties ("The National Historic Preservation Act". 2016; "Section 106: Frequently Asked Questions" 2012).

What is a Historic Property?

A historic property is any site listed or eligible for listing on the National Register of Historic Places (NRHP), which itself is a part of the National Park Services. To be added to the NRHP, a property must meet at least one of the following criteria:
  • Criterion A - The property is associated with a historically significant event.
  • Criterion B - The property is associated with a historically significant person.
  • Criterion C - The property is emblematic of a historically significant style.
  • Criterion D - The property has yielded or is likely to yield historically significant information.
It is also expected that State Historic Preservation Officers and Tribal Historic Preservation Officers are consulted to ensure all possible historic sites are identified.

Background of Section 106

Section 106 as it appears in NHPA

§306108. Effect of undertaking on historic property

“The head of any Federal agency having direct or indirect jurisdiction over a proposed Federal or federally assisted undertaking in any State and the head of any Federal department or independent agency having authority to license any undertaking, prior to the approval of the expenditure of any Federal funds on the undertaking or prior to the issuance of any license, hall take into account the effect of the undertaking on any historic property. The head of the Federal agency shall afford the Council a reasonable opportunity to comment with regard to the undertaking.”

Purpose of Section 106

Section 106 dictates that federal agencies or federally associated programs must recognize and identify the effect of any undertaking near historic property on or eligible for the National Register. This submission must allow a reasonable opportunity for State Historic Preservation Officers, tribes, and other affected communities the opportunity to comment in the form of consultation. Although the effect on potential historic properties must be recognized there is nothing preventing continuation of the undertaking despite adverse effects

History of Section 106

Section 106 was enacted in 1966 as part of the National Historic Preservation Act (NHPA), in response to the loss of several historic sites to urban expansion. The community was not involved directly in the development of Section 106, but as public engagement is an overall priority of the NHPA, it is by extension a priority of Section 106.

Impacts of Section 106

Dakota Acess Pipeline

Likely one of the best known examples of Section 106, the Dakota Access Pipeline is a 1,172-mile-long underground pipeline, transporting crude oil from North Dakota to Illinois, where it connects with the Energy Transfer Crude Oil Pipeline to refineries in Texas, together forming the transcontinental Bakken Pipeline(Hu 2024).

As the pipeline’s planned route involved hundreds of water crossings, the company behind its construction, Energy Transfer LP, was required by the Rivers and Harbors Act and the Clean Water Act to acquire permits with the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE), which resulted in Section 106 taking effect. However, the U.S. Army Corps of Engineers is unique from other federal agencies in that its application of the Section 106 process follows their own regulations, which have not been approved by the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation and have long been decried as insufficient in meeting the demands of the National Historic Preservation Act (Steurer 2025).

Under their own regulations, the USACE argued that it was only in the permit areas that they had to assess their impacts on historic properties, rather than along the entire 1,172-mile length of the pipeline. This decision led to conflict with the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation, who argued that the USACE failed to fulfill their responsibilities under Section 106 in their refusal to recognize the Dakota Access Pipeline as a single undertaking, and in their insufficient tribal consultation (Steurer 2025). The Standing Rock Sioux Tribe in particular criticized the construction of the pipeline and the USACE’s handling of the Section 106 process. As the pipeline’ planned route would put it under Lake Oahe, directly upstream of the Standing Rock reservation, its construction would risk contaminating their water supply, along with many sacred sites (Hu 2024).



In July 2016, the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe attempted to sue the USACE and request an emergency stop to the pipeline’s construction, citing their violation of the Clean Water Act, NEPA, and NHPA. However, the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia ruled in favor of the USACE, asserting that they were not liable to conduct a full environmental impact statement due to the majority of the construction being on private land (Hu 2024). The following appeal by the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe was also denied. Immediately after this ruling, the Departments of Justice, the Interior, and the Army issued a Joint Statement, asserting that construction within a 20-mile radius of Lake Oahe would not continue until the Army had sufficiently determined whether their prior decisions were in accordance with NEPA and other such federal laws. However, this statement only required that Energy Transfer LP voluntarily pause construction of the pipeline (Compton 2016), which they did not (Hu 2024).

It was only in December 2016 that the USACE denied the easement needed for the Lake Oahe crossing, citing conflicts with treaty and tribal water rights. The USACE also announced that it would conduct an environmental impact statement (EIS), and potentially even consider alternative routes for the Dakota Access Pipeline. However, a 2017 Presidential Memorandum directed the easement approval be expedited, leading the USACE to cancel the EIS and approve the easement. By April of 2017, the Dakota Access Pipeline was complete. While the Standing Rock Sioux Tribe would continue to fight against the pipeline in court, even being successful in revoking the USACE’s easement and forcing them to conduct their EIS, the Dakota Access Pipeline remains fully operational to this day (Hu 2024).

Nine Mile Canyon

Nine Mile Canyon, located in Eastern Utah, is known for its many historic properties, most notably its extensive prehistoric rock art, spread across roughly 10,000 panels comprising over 100,000 individual images, all of which bear immense significance to the local Ute people. However, the discovery of natural gas reserves in the region led the energy company Bill Barrett Corporation, in collaboration with the Bureau of Land Management, to propose an 800-well natural gas development in 2005.

The development of the well would lead to an increase in road traffic, which led to concerns of debris and pollutants kicked up by motor vehicles eroding the petroglyphs. To combat this, the Bill Barrett Corporation introduced magnesium chloride-based dust suppressants to dampen the roads and prevent dust from covering the artwork. Unfortunately, magnesium chloride is corrosive and clings to rock, further damaging the petroglyphs.

As a federal agency, the Bureau of Land Management was required by Section 106 to consider the effect of the well on the petroglyphs. While at first the Bureau only engaged in consultation on a limited scale, protest from activists, archaeologists, and Native Americans, as well as encouragement from the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation, eventually prompted them to expand from consultation to collaboration in 2008.

After months of negotiation, an agreement was finally settled in 2010. While the project would proceed as planned, the Bureau of Land Management would fund additional archaeological surveys in the area to identify 100 new historic properties, and informative panels would be installed in Nile Mile Canyon. Additionally, the use of the corrosive dust suppressants was forbidden, and research into the impacts of such airborne pollutants on rock art and potential conservation treatments to address them would be performed. The case of Nine Mile Canyon had a significant impact on Section 106 itself, as it set a precedent regarding consultation and when it should occur during an undertaking.


African Burial Ground

In 1987, the General Services Administration proposed a plan for the Ted Weiss Federal Building to be constructed in Lower Manhattan, New York City. The site was known to have several structures built overtop of each other over the centuries, and it was anticipated that there may be remnants of a cemetery located in an adjacent alley. However, pre-construction surveys in 1991 led to the discovery of a startling number of human remains.

What the surveys had uncovered was a site that had historically been known as the “Negros Buriel Ground (sic),” a mass cemetery predominantly for enslaved people of African descent, used between 1650 and 1794. Realizing the significance of the discovery, Section 106 came into effect, requiring the General Services Administration engage in consultation regarding the project.

However, the General Services Administration continued to underestimate the sheer scope of the African Burial Ground. As such, consultation was relatively limited, and the construction project was set to continue unchanged. However, the continued discoveries of remains, now numbering hundreds of individuals, led to increasing concern over the project, prompting the Advisory Council on Historic Preservation to intervene and push for a change in plans.

In 1992, an onsite congressional hearing determined that work on the project should be halted until an agreement was reached. Consultation expanded to include dozens of groups, including the General Services Administration, National Park Service, senator of New York, and the descendant community. It was eventually agreed that the footprint of the Ted Weiss Federal Building would be altered to accommodate the African Burial Ground, and the 419 individuals uncovered would be re-interred through a collaborative effort between the academic and descendant communities. The African Burial Ground would be designated as a National Historic Landmark in 1993, and as a National Monument in 2006.

Through Section 106, the African Burial Ground was remade into a monument commemorating both the past and present struggles of African Americans. Additionally, the discovery of the site revolutionized the understanding of slavery in early New York, which had previously been largely forgotten.



Improvements

Section 106 is a strong start to preserving historic sites in the United States, unfortunately the legislation put in place by section 106 does not fully ensure these sites will be preserved, only for the impact of an undertaking on the site to be considered. Thus an undertaking can fully destroy a site with no legal repercussions so long as the destruction is considered during the evaluation and development of the project.

To improve section 106 legislation there are several things to consider. The first major concern with section 106 is actually about how to go about executing section 106. Currently in the United States there is no national standard for how section 106 investigations are to occur. This is partially understandable considering the variety present in sites, environments, and projects in different parts of the country. Even with the level of variety present efforts can still be taken to improve the regulations associated with section 106 projects.
